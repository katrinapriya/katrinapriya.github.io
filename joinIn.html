When wanting to do something with friends, it can be frustrating to figure out who to reach out to. It’s difficult to initiate activities when you have to keep bugging friends to check availability and interest. Once I realized how frequently I face this problem, I became determined to find a solution. To address this problem, I designed an app where interested and available friends come to you.


Problem



Research & Analysis

I wanted to see if others faced this problem, and to what extent. I interviewed 10 young adults and conducted research on Reddit and Twitter to learn more about frustrations around wanting to do something with friends.

I used affinity diagramming to cluster observations from interviews & research into insights.


Here are a few of the insights I gathered:

  • Planning activities with friends requires a lot of effort and time. It's difficult to commit to a time that works for everyone. It can be hard to initiate, especially in group settings.

  • Availability of friends is important, but often unknown. There's no way to actually know who's available without repeatedly messaging first. Slow communication limits action, especially when there's a time constraint.

  • With fewer in-person interactions during the COVID-19 pandemic, there is less knowledge of friends' availabilities. People are feeling a greater lack of motivation and accountability for social interactions.

Ideation

I was interested in designing a solution that I could actually develop myself. I have some iOS app development experience, so I chose to design an app.

I brainstormed app features based on the key insights I extracted from the interviews.


I drew my visual design inspiration from apps that are popular among college students today, such as Venmo, Snapchat, and Instagram.

I took note of visual elements that I wanted to replicate in my own design.


Prototyping

I began with basic low-fidelity sketches.


I developed the sketches into mid-fidelity wireframes focused on layout.

Throughout the prototyping process, I asked users for feedback. With their help, I designed an app with the following features:

  • A feed that lets you browse through what your friends are up to. Joining an activity just takes one click. I wanted the primary functions of the app to be straightforward.

  • The ability to indicate whether or not you are available to do something with friends. If you are available, you will get notified of friends’ posts; otherwise, you won’t be bothered. This way, you won't get ghosted by busy friends.

  • A groups feature that notifies you when your whole group is available. Intervieees complained about planning activities with groups, as it can be difficult to plan around multiple schedules.

User Testing

I watched users interact with the prototype and listened to whether it solves their frustrations. In terms of usability, they found the design easy-to-use and intuitive. A common topic of concern was about how this app would help plan details after joining an activity. I'm considering implementing polls & a restaurant picker that incorporates each group member’s preferences to address this.

After another design iteration, I plan on beginning to code and develop the actual app.


Many users were very excited about the idea and saw themselves using it.


Takeaways

  • Don't ask users to design features; instead, listen to their pain points and see if you can address them.
  • It helps to write down your values and vision for an idea, so that you can reference it while making design decisions. This is especially important when you have contradictory interview or research data.
  • Start simple - there's no need to address every single frustration of every potential user.
