Problem: I often want to do something with friends, but don't know who would be available and interested.

Solution: To address this problem, I designed an app where interested and available friends come to you.

Problem





Research & Analysis

I wanted to see if others faced this problem, and to what extent. I interviewed college students and conducted research on Reddit and Twitter to learn more about user pain points.

I used affinity diagramming to cluster observations from interviews & research into insights.


Here are a few of the user insights:

  1. Planning activities with friends requires a lot of effort and time. It's difficult to commit to a time that works for everyone. It can be hard to initiate, especially in group settings.

  2. Availability of friends is important, but often unknown. There's no way to actually know who's available without repeatedly messaging first. Slow communication limits action, especially when there's a time constraint.

  3. With fewer in-person interactions during the COVID-19 pandemic, there is less knowledge of friends' availabilities. People are feeling a greater lack of motivation and accountability for social interactions.



Ideation

I was interested in designing a solution that I could develop myself. I have some iOS app development experience, so I chose to design an app.

I brainstormed app features & design goals based on the key insights I extracted from the interviews.


I drew my visual design inspiration from apps that are popular among college students today, such as Venmo, Snapchat, and Instagram.


I took note of visual elements that I wanted to replicate in my own design.




Prototyping

Throughout the prototyping process, I asked users for feedback. With their help, I designed an app with the following features:

  1. A feed that lets you browse through what your friends are up to. Joining an activity just takes one click. I wanted the primary function of the app to be straightforward.

  2. The ability to indicate whether or not you are available to do something with friends. If you are available, you will get notified of friends’ posts; otherwise, you won’t be bothered. This way, you won't get ghosted by busy friends.

  3. A groups feature that notifies you when your whole group is available. Interviewees complained about planning activities with groups, as it can be difficult to plan around multiple schedules.

I turned sketches into mid-fidelity wireframes focused on layout.

User Testing

I asked questions and had users think out loud while going through the high-fidelity app. Users found the design to be easy-to-use and intuitive.

Many users were very excited about the idea and saw themselves using it.




Next Steps

After another design iteration where I address additional details, I plan on beginning to code and develop the actual app.

Eventually, I can measure the success of this app using the following metrics:

  1. Engagement - are users engaged with this app and using it frequently? Look at average session frequency & intervals, and number of active users.
  2. Happiness - how satisfied and happy are users with joinIn? Observe net promoter scores and number of 5-star ratings.
  3. Retention - do users keep coming back to use the app? Calculate churn rate & retention rate.




Takeaways

  1. Don't ask users to design features; instead, listen to their pain points and see if you can address them through your design.
  2. I definitely value new perspectives on this project, as I've been working on it by myself for so long. Going forward, I hope to include more people into the design process.
  3. Start simple - there's no need to address every single frustration of every potential user.
