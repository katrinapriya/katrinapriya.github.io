We worked with Sayfty, an organization that aims to empower women by providing resources to victims of sexual assault and domestic violence in India. One of my team members was more focused on user research, while the other member and I focused on design & prototyping.

Problem: Users had trouble finding what they were looking for in Sayfty's existing Android app.

Solution: A redesigned app with an informative onboarding process and more intuitive home screen & menu layouts.


App Goals

We started with creating a design brief that included business and user goals for the app.




Current App (before redesign)

Here are screenshots of the existing app before our redesign. The app aims to provide resources and information to women affected by gender violence.


Our first major design decision was choosing which area of the app to focus on. We conducted usability tests and cognitive walkthroughs with the current app to determine areas that needed to be improved. We then put our findings in a user journey map.



User Journey Map



We learned from our journey map that users were struggling the most with the onboarding process and navigating the home screen. Our goal then became to help users understand how to use the various features of the app and find important resources and information easily.




Design Goals


We defined these design goals from the journey map insights.




Initial Sketches & Wireframes

We created sketches and wireframes based on our design goals.


Our early wireframes were primarily focused on the home screen, as that is the most central and crucial page of the app. We also prioritized urgent support, since we identified earlier that it wasn't easily accessible in the current app.




User Testing Insights

Next, we conducted user tests with our mid-fidelity designs; here are some of the key insights we gained.


We discovered that most users will regularly be using the app for non-urgent resources, so we should actually be prioritizing that, while still making urgent support easily accessible. Another realization was that women in India are often concerned about involving the police in urgent situations, and would rather contact emergency contacts.

From user testing, we found that several of the earlier issues we found still existed in our latest iteration, along with new issues. We addressed these points in our next iteration.



Home Screen Iterations

Based on user feedback, we created higher fidelity mockups of different home screen ideas.


We learned from user tests that we needed visual hierarchy on the home screen. We eventually combined the second and fourth screens above to highlight the most important feature: the Survivors' Toolkit.



Style Guide





Final Prototype

I was the primary designer for the screens below (home, toolkit, onboarding). My team member also designed a meditiation feature for users who may feel overwhelmed while using the app. You can interact with all of these elements in our final Figma interactive prototype.


My team and I presented our work to the Sayfty team and to classmates at UC Berkeley's School of Information. They were impressed with our design work, especially the user insights we gathered.

Sayfty is now working on implementing our designs for the next version of their app.




Takeaways

  1. It's important to prioritize certain features or elements on a screen. Visual hierarchy helps guide the user and highlights the most crucial feature.
  2. Be aware and mindful of cultural differences while designing for an international audience. Instead of making assumptions, ask and listen!
  3. Working with a user researcher, I learned a lot about conducting user interviews. I discovered new types of questions to ask and practiced being more patient, observant, and empathetic.
