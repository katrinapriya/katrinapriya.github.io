Sayfty aims to empower women by providing resources to victims of sexual assault and domestic violence in India. I led design & prototyping efforts in collaboration with another designer and a user researcher.

Problem: User research found that Sayftyâ€™s app users were unclear on how to navigate the features of the app.

Solution: A redesigned app with an informative onboarding process and more intuitive home screen & menu layouts.


App Goals

I started with creating a design brief that included business and user goals for the app.




Current App (before redesign)

The app aims to provide both urgent and long-term resources to women affected by gender violence.


The first major design decision was choosing which area of the app to focus on. My team and I conducted usability tests and cognitive walkthroughs with the current app to determine areas that needed to be improved. We then put our findings in a user journey map.




User Journey Map



The user journey map indicated users were struggling with onboarding and navigation (especially with the home screen). Based on this, the goal became to help users understand features and easily find important information & resources.




Design Goals


I defined these design goals from the journey map insights in order to help onboarding & navigation.




Initial Sketches & Wireframes

We created sketches and wireframes based on our design goals.


The early wireframes were primarily focused on the home screen, as that is the most central and crucial page of the app. We also prioritized urgent support, since we identified earlier that it wasn't easily accessible in the current app.




User Testing Insights

Here are key insights gained from user testing mid-fidelity designs.


We discovered that most users will regularly be using the app for non-urgent resources, so we should actually be prioritizing that, while still making urgent support accessible.

Another learning was that women in India are often concerned about involving the police, and would feel safer contacting emergency contacts.



Home Screen Iterations

In response to user feedback, I simplified the navigation bar and designed various layouts for the home screen.


User tests illustrated a need for visual hierarchy on the home screen. I learned to prioritize the most important feature of the app: the Survivors' Toolkit.

Results

My team and I presented our work to the Sayfty team and to students at UC Berkeley's School of Information.

Sayfty was impressed by our design work, and is now working on implementing our designs for the next version of the app.

Once implemented, here are some metrics I would measure our design's success by:

  1. Adoption - does the new onboarding process result in more regular users? See if the ratio between registration rate and download rate has improved.
  2. Task success - can users quickly and easily find what they are looking for on the app? User test various tasks and track how long it takes to complete them & the success rate.
  3. Usefulness - has the redesign made the app more useful to those seeking resources? Look at # of 5-star app store reviews & number of calls made through the app.




Takeaways

  1. It's important to prioritize certain features or elements on a screen. Visual hierarchy helps guide the user and highlights the most crucial feature.
  2. Be aware and mindful of cultural differences while designing for an international audience. Instead of making assumptions, ask and listen!
  3. Working with a user researcher, I learned a lot about conducting user interviews. I discovered new types of questions to ask and practiced being more patient, observant, and empathetic.
