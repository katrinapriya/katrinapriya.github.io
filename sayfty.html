Sayfty aims to empower women by providing resources to victims of sexual assault and domestic violence in India. I led design & prototyping efforts in collaboration with another designer and a user researcher.

Problem: User research found that users were having trouble finding what they were looking for in Sayfty's existing app.

Solution: A redesigned app with an informative onboarding process and more intuitive home screen & menu layouts.


App Goals

I started with creating a design brief that included business and user goals for the app.




Current App (before redesign)

The app aims to provide resources and information to women affected by gender violence.


The first major design decision was choosing which area of the app to focus on. My team and I conducted usability tests and cognitive walkthroughs with the current app to determine areas that needed to be improved. We then put our findings in a user journey map.



User Journey Map



The user journey map indicated users were struggling with onboarding and navigation. Based on this, the goal became to help users understand features and easily find important information & resources.




Design Goals


I defined these design goals from the journey map insights.




Initial Sketches & Wireframes

We created sketches and wireframes based on our design goals.


The early wireframes were primarily focused on the home screen, as that is the most central and crucial page of the app. We also prioritized urgent support, since we identified earlier that it wasn't easily accessible in the current app.




User Testing Insights

Here are key insights gained from user tests with the mid-fidelity designs.


We discovered that most users will regularly be using the app for non-urgent resources, so we should actually be prioritizing that, while still making urgent support accessible. Another learning was that women in India are often concerned about involving the police, and would feel safer contacting emergency contacts.

From user testing, we found that several of the earlier issues still existed in our latest iteration, along with some new concerns. I addressed these points in our next iteration.



Home Screen Iterations

Based on user feedback, we created higher fidelity mockups of different home screen ideas.


User tests illustrated a need for visual hierarchy on the home screen. I eventually combined the second and fourth screens above to highlight the most important feature: the Survivors' Toolkit.

Results

My team and I presented our work to the Sayfty team and to classmates at UC Berkeley's School of Information. They were impressed with our design work, especially the user insights we gathered.

Sayfty is now working on implementing our designs for the next version of their app.

Once implemented, here are some metrics to measure our design's success by:

  1. Adoption - does the new onboarding process help more people become regular users? See if the ratio between registration rate and download rate has improved.
  2. Task success - can users quickly and easily find what they are looking for on the app? User test various scenarios and track how long it takes to complete them & the success rate.




Takeaways

  1. It's important to prioritize certain features or elements on a screen. Visual hierarchy helps guide the user and highlights the most crucial feature.
  2. Be aware and mindful of cultural differences while designing for an international audience. Instead of making assumptions, ask and listen!
  3. Working with a user researcher, I learned a lot about conducting user interviews. I discovered new types of questions to ask and practiced being more patient, observant, and empathetic.
