We worked with Sayfty, an organization that aims to empower women by providing resources to victims of sexual assault and domestic violence in India. One of my team members was more focused on user research, while the other member and I focused on design & prototyping.

We redesigned Sayfty's existing Android app to help users better understand the features of the app, and to improve overall navigation.

We started with creating a design brief that included business and user goals.



Here are screenshots of the existing app before our redesign.


Our first major design decision was choosing which area of the app to focus on. We conducted usability tests and cognitive walkthroughs with the current app to determine areas that needed to be improved. We then put our findings in a user journey map.




We learned from our journey map that users were struggling the most with the onboarding process and navigating the home screen. Our goal became to help users understand how to use the various features of the app and to find important resources and information easily.




These are the design goals we had going into prototyping.



We created sketches and wireframes based on our design goals.


Our early wireframes were primarily focused on the home screen, as that is the most central and important page of the app. We prioritized urgent support, since that wasn't easily accessible with the current app.



Here are some of the key insights we got from user testing our mid-fidelity designs.


We discovered that most users will regularly be using the app for non-urgent resources, so we should actually be prioritizing that, while still making urgent support easily accessible. Another realization was that women in India are often concerned about involving the police in urgent situations, and would rather contact emergency contacts.

From user testing, we found that several of the earlier issues we found still existed in our latest iteration, along with new issues. We addressed these points in our next iteration.


Based on user feedback, we created higher fidelity mockups of different home screen ideas.


We learned from user tests that we needed visual hierarchy on the home screen. We eventually combined the 2nd and 4th screens above to highlight the most important feature: the Survivors' Toolkit.



Along with the home screen and toolkit page, we also updated the onboarding process and added a meditiation feature for users who may feel overwhelmed using the app. You can view our final Figma prototype here.
