Background

Smart home devices are becoming increasingly popular in the US. These internet-connected devices range from smart thermostats and speakers to security cameras and voice-enabled assistants such as Amazon Echo and Google Home. Unfortunately, the majority of these devices don't have privacy and security by design. My team and I wanted to bring security, privacy, and transparency to the smart home device experience.


Research & Analysis

We conducted a total of 16 interviews with smart home device users, experts, and stakeholders. Our interviews illustrated a lack of urgency for strong security and privacy measures from users, who often undervalue their data. This lack of urgency exists in device makers as well, since they are not being held accountable for their poor security standards.

We used affinity diagramming to extract common themes from our interviews.

Smart home device users were instead focused on convenience and accessibility. It became clear to us that to get users to care about cybersecurity, we must make security accessible and convenient. We reframed our project to solve the following question: How might we make security and privacy accessible and essential to smart home device consumers?


Ideation

We brainstormed solutions and created clusters of common themes. Using those clusters, we converged to 3 solutions.

After discussing our ideas, we felt that redesigning a smart home device could make the device less convenient and usable as we replace features. Meanwhile, our second option was dependent on the financial capabilities of smart home device companies and users, which reduces its accessibility.

We chose to pursue the privacy & security label, as it provides the most accessibility to consumers, while also making security more essential for the device makers. With this label, we hope to empower customers to evaluate privacy and security features before purchasing a smart home device.


Prototyping

To make the label's content relevant, we researched aspects of privacy and security that should concern users. We created the initial prototypes after making the following design choices:

  • We used a table layout to keep the information organized and easy-to-read. In terms of design, we were inspired by the Nutrition Facts label on food boxes today.

    • We started with sketches, then moved on to a digital prototype, which was then laser cut on plywood.


  • We added a QR code that links to a webpage explaining concepts from the label that might be unknown to a user. This is to increase the accessibility of security concepts to an average smart home device users.

    • We each came up with our own designs for the webpage from the QR scan.


  • We included face icons to illustrate level of caution for users with less knowledge of security, and for those who look briefly at the label. This aims to make the label more convenient, since consumers of smart home devices prioritize convenience.


Final Design

Through the insights gained from usability testing of our designs, I created our final prototype.


After receiving user feedback, I made the following design changes:

  • I made the label smaller. Once we had the physical prototype in our hands, we realized that the label was too big to be put on smaller device boxes. It was a challenge to fit the same information in a smaller label, so I had to make some subtle design changes.

  • I expanded the face icons to every row on the label, instead of just the security section. Users found the emojis extremely useful, so I made sure to utilize it throughout the sticker.

  • I made the webpage more user-friendly by combining the best assets of each of our designs. Drop down menus and large chunks of text were not accessible to users. I designed the layout to be similar to a phone's Settings page, so that it feels familiar to users. I kept it clean and monochromatic to match the label's design.

You can interact with our webpage prototype, view our Behance page, and enjoy our YouTube video showing the label in use!


Takeaways

  • It's very important to keep security and privacy in mind while designing products.
  • It not enough to just inform users about important issues like cybersecurity, you have to also get them to care.
  • Empathy goes a long way in interviewing and understanding users.
  • Trust your teammates and be flexible when things don't go your way.
  • Talking to experts in a field you don't know much about can be intimidating. By researching the topics beforehand, you can be prepared to make the most out of the interview!
